# hardiness — the crop's cold tolerance (and the site's zone)

**hardiness** is cold tolerance, with two faces. The **crop trait**: **hardy** (tolerates hard frost ~25–28 °F — kale, spinach, leek), **half-hardy** (light frost only — cauliflower, lettuce, chard), **tender** (injured near freezing — tomato, pepper, cucurbit), **very-tender** (needs warm nights — melon, okra). The **place trait**: the **USDA hardiness zone** (1–13) bins a site by its average annual minimum temperature, siting perennials and overwintering.

Hardiness is the biological complement to the [[frost]] bound and the built [[tunnel]] protection — a hardy crop *is* its own season extension, shrugging off the frost a tender crop dies to. It sets which crops anchor the spring and fall [[harvest]] waves of the [[season]] and which can overwinter. It is a classification ([[taxonomy]]-adjacent trait) the [[planting]] calendar reads.

## Standards
- USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map (2023 — the 13-zone minimum-temperature bands)
- Penn State / extension — hardy / half-hardy / tender vegetable classification; RHS hardiness ratings
