# postharvest — handling produce between harvest and sale

**postharvest** is everything between the [[harvest]] event and the sale — **cooling → washing → curing → [[grade|grading]] → [[packs|packing]] → storage** — the chain that preserves quality. Its first law: postharvest **cannot improve quality, only slow its loss**, so speed matters. **Field heat** (the crop's temperature at harvest) drives respiration and decay, so **precooling** (forced-air, hydro, vacuum, room, or ice) removes it fast; **washing** (triple-wash + spin-dry for greens) cleans; **curing** (warm-hold for onions/garlic/winter-squash to dry skins and convert starch→sugar) readies storage crops; then the unbroken **cold chain** holds quality through storage and transport.

The conserved gap between gross and marketable [[yield]] — **shrink** (transpiration weight loss + culls + decay) — is lost here; minimizing it is the point. Postharvest is the bridge from [[biological/assets|field asset]] to sellable [[items|inventory]] ([[commerce]]); under FSMA it is also the food-safety surface (agricultural water, the cold chain). It composes [[grade]] and [[packs]].

## Standards
- UC Davis Postharvest Technology Center (cooling methods, the cold chain, produce facts); SARE *Wholesale Success* (packing)
- FDA FSMA Produce Safety Rule — 21 CFR Part 112 (harvesting/packing/holding, agricultural water)
- *Knott's Handbook for Vegetable Growers* — postharvest & storage conditions
